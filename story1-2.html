The Legend of Ube, the Gift of the Earth

Long ago, before the Chocolate Hills rose like sleeping giants and before rivers learned the paths they now follow, the island of Bohol was watched over by the spirits of the land. These spirits lived beneath the soil, in the roots of trees and stones, and they listened closely to the hearts of the people.

In a small farming village near the coast lived a young girl named Amihan. She was quiet but kind, and every morning she helped her mother tend their garden. The land was dry, and food was scarce. Many crops withered under the hot sun, and hunger visited the village like an unwanted guest.

One day, while digging the earth with her wooden spade, Amihan heard a soft voice.

“Dig gently,” it whispered. “The earth feels pain, too.”

Startled, Amihan stopped. From the soil emerged a diwata—a spirit of the land—her hair the color of twilight and her skin glowing like moonlight on water.

“You are the first who has listened,” said the diwata. “Because of your care for the land, I will give your people a gift.”

The diwata touched the ground, and from the soil grew a plant with wide green leaves. Beneath it lay a root unlike any the villagers had seen—deep purple, rich as the evening sky.

“This is ube,” the diwata said. “It will grow strong beneath the earth, even when the sun is harsh. Treat it with patience, and it will feed many.”

Amihan shared the gift with the village. The people planted ube carefully, never rushing the harvest, always thanking the land. When the yams were finally dug up, they were plentiful and sweet. Mothers cooked them into warm dishes, elders shared them during gatherings, and children laughed with purple-stained smiles.

But the diwata warned them, “Remember, ube grows hidden. Like true blessings, it requires time, humility, and respect.”

The villagers remembered. They learned that strength does not always show above ground, and that the earth gives generously when treated with care.

To this day, the people of Bohol believe that every ube carries the spirit of patience and gratitude. Its purple color reminds them of the sky at dusk—when day rests and the land quietly prepares for tomorrow.

And so, whenever ube is shared during fiestas or family meals, the elders smile and say:

“This is not just food. This is the earth remembering our kindness.”
